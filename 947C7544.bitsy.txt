24 July. Whitby - Lucy met me at the station, & we drove up to the house in which they have rooms. This is a lovely place. The little river, the Esk, runs through a deep valley, which broadens out as it comes near the harbour.

DLG ITM_5
Between the abbey & the town there is a church, round which is a big graveyard, all full of tombstones. This is to my mind the nicest spot in Whitby, for it lies right over the town, & has a full view of the harbour & all up the bay to where the headland called Kettleness stretches out into the sea. It descends so steeply over the harbour that part of the bank has fallen away, & some of the graves have been destroyed. In one place part of the stonework of the graves stretches out over the sandy pathway far below.

DLG ITM_6
The harbour lies below me. It is nice at high water; but when the tide is out it shoals away to nothing, & here is merely the stream of the Esk, running between banks of sand, with rocks here and there.

DLG ITM_7
Right over the town is the ruin of Whitby Abbey, which was sacked by the Danes, and which is the scene of part of "Marmion", where the girl was built up in the wall. It is a most noble ruin, of immense size, and full of beautiful and romantic bits; there is a legend that a white lady is seen in one of the windows.

High tide had come by the time I reached rocks of the shoreline. Outside the harbour on this side there rises for about half a mile a great reef, the sharp edge of which runs straight out from behind the south lighthouse. At the end of it is a buoy with a bell, which swings in bad weather, and sends in a mournful sound on the wind. They have a legend here that when a ship is lost bells are heard out at sea.

I arrived at the place where I had seen the lights from across the harbour. Here a large group of men were clambering over a ruined schooner caught out in the storm & pitched up unceremoniously on The Sear an hour or so before I arrived. Their looks were confused, & I overheard talk of the dead captain tied to the wheel bearing a crucifix & no-one else aboard. The cargo they mentioned was unusual to me - silver sand and earth. "Where that huge dog is now, & how it survived that ungodly storm I will never no," one of them said with unease in his voice. I stayed until it was light, & then headed to the station to meet my fiance Jonathan, who I was expecting on the early train.
